Marketing your pop-up

A simple weekly rhythm that actually drives foot traffic

You don't need a marketing strategy. You need a repeatable weekly habit that keeps your existing audience aware you exist in person — and helps local people who don't know you yet find you.

The core principle

Consistency beats creativity. One post every Thursday morning for six months will outperform sporadic bursts of elaborate content. The algorithm rewards regularity, and your audience learns to expect it. Pick a rhythm you can actually sustain, then stick to it.

A simple weekly posting rhythm

Monday

Optional

Product or behind-the-scenes post

A photo of something you're making, restocking, or preparing for the weekend. Builds anticipation without announcing anything yet. Good for Instagram or Facebook.

Thursday

Essential

"We're open this weekend" reminder

Short, specific, every week. Mention one product. Include your hours and location. Tag @CityofBothell. This is the post that actually drives people to show up — don't skip it.

Saturday

Recommended

In-the-moment story or post

"We're here right now — come find us on Main Street." A photo of your setup that day. This catches people who are already out and looking for something to do. Stories work well for this.

Sunday eve

Optional

Weekend recap

One sentence about how the weekend went. What sold out? Who came by? What was a highlight? This closes the loop for followers who saw your Thursday post and builds momentum for next week.

Using the city's social channels

Tag the city in your posts

When you tag @CityofBothell in a post about your pop-up, the city can reshare it to their followers — giving you access to an audience you don't have yet. This is free reach. Use it every Thursday post at minimum.

Use #PopShopsOnMain consistently

This hashtag builds a collective feed of all vendor content from the program. When the city promotes Pop Shops generally, people who follow the hashtag see all vendor posts — including yours. It compounds over time as more vendors use it.

Promotions — how to run one without undermining yourself

If you run a promotion, do it right

  • Always tie a promotion to a specific reason — a new product launch, a holiday weekend, your anniversary in the program. "Just because" discounts feel random and cheapen your brand.
  • Set a clear end date. "This weekend only" creates urgency. An open-ended discount is just a lower price.
  • Promote one thing, not everything. A discount on one item drives curiosity and foot traffic without cutting into your overall margin.
  • Track whether it actually worked. Did you sell more that weekend than your average? If not, the promotion cost you margin for no traffic gain — and you should try something different next time.

The trap to avoid

Posting only when you feel inspired. Marketing works through repetition and expectation, not quality alone. Your audience needs to see you regularly to remember to visit. Set a calendar reminder every Thursday morning and post something — even if it's just a quick photo of your setup. Showing up consistently is more valuable than occasional great content.
