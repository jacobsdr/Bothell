Setting up your space

How to make your pop-up feel intentional — not improvised

You know your product. The hard part is that an 8×10 foot space can feel like a cluttered closet or a focused boutique — and the difference often comes down to a few small decisions made before you open on day one.

The 3-second rule — do this before every weekend

Step outside your space and walk toward it as if you've never seen it before. What's the first thing your eye goes to? Is it your best, most visually interesting product — or a pile of inventory bags, a price list, or the back of a display rack? If it's not your hero product, fix that before you open.

"The chalkboard has helped a lot in the last month — adding it made the space feel visibly open. The door needs to be opened. People won't stop if they can't tell anyone's there."

— Cozy Cabin Crafting, Pop Shops on Main vendor

Five fundamentals for a small space

01

Create one clear focal point — your hero product

Every well-merchandised space has one thing that anchors it. Your hero should be visually distinctive, elevated, and easy to see from outside the space. It tells people what you do faster than any sign could. It doesn't have to be your most expensive item — it should be the one that best represents your brand and creates curiosity.

Try: For a custom embroidery vendor, one large finished piece works better than a rack of smaller items.

02

Control eye level — vary your heights

The zone between a customer's waist and eyes is your selling zone. Products placed too low get overlooked; too high feel inaccessible. Use risers, shelving, or small crates to vary heights and create visual depth. A flat table of products is hard to read at a glance.

Try: A simple wooden crate or a stack of books under a cloth riser costs almost nothing and changes the look immediately.

03

Make pricing visible — always

No one wants to ask how much something costs. If they have to ask, many won't — they'll put it down and walk away. Every item, or every clearly defined group of items, needs a visible price. Clear, handwritten tags often perform better than printed labels in a market setting.

Try: A small label near each item, plus one larger sign showing your range (e.g. "Items from $12–$45") for people browsing from a distance.

04

Group by story, not by category

Instead of displaying all your candles together and all your soaps together, try grouping items that tell the same story. A "Sunday morning" vignette with a candle, a soap, and a small card conveys a lifestyle — and customers buy the whole story, not just the item. This also makes your space feel curated rather than like a catalog.

Try: Pick one theme per surface — "gift for her," "everyday essential," "weekend ritual" — and group accordingly.

05

Leave breathing room

The instinct in a small space is to fill every inch with inventory. Resist it. A cramped display makes customers feel like they'll knock something over — so they don't touch anything. A rule of thumb: if everything is touching, remove one third of the items. You'll likely sell more.

Try: Remove a third of your display items and see if the space feels more considered and easier to browse.

06

Signal that you're open

A closed door, no vendor visible, and no exterior signage reads as closed — even during business hours. Keep your door open or propped. Put a chalkboard outside with something handwritten — your hours, a featured item, a friendly message. And stand near the entrance, not behind your table — your visible presence makes a huge difference.

You can place signage and displays up to 5 feet outside your unit. Use it — a display table or chalkboard can catch foot traffic that would otherwise walk past.

Exterior space rule

You are allowed to place signage and displays up to 5 feet outside your unit. Use this. A chalkboard or small display table in front of your space catches foot traffic that would otherwise walk straight past.

Before your first day — setup checklist

Run through this before you open

  • Hero product identified and placed at eye level, visible from outside the unit
  • All items priced and prices visible without picking up the item
  • Products grouped by story or theme, not just by type
  • Display has visual depth — at least two height levels
  • Breathing room between products — nothing is touching everything else
  • Exterior signage or chalkboard ready and placed up to 5 feet outside your unit
  • Payment method tested and working (Square, Tap to Pay, Venmo)
  • You've done the 3-second test — walked up as a stranger and checked the first impression
