Phase 1 of 3

Learn

Months 1–6 of your two-year program

Your only job right now is to pay attention. Don't rearrange everything, don't change your prices, don't stress about optimizing. This phase is about gathering real information about how your business works in a physical space.

The core principle of Phase 1

Resist the urge to fix things immediately. You can't improve what you don't yet understand. A slow Saturday in month two isn't a failure — it's data. Your goal is to end month six with a clear picture of what's actually happening, not what you assumed would happen.

"The in-person dialogue helps me a lot. I get orders from all over the world online — but people won't know the quality unless they can feel it. Shipping costs more than the item sometimes. Being here lets me understand what actually sells and why."

— Cozy Cabin Crafting, Pop Shops on Main vendor

What to pay attention to

What sells — and what doesn't?

Track which items move each weekend. Are your bestsellers online the same as in-person? You'll often be surprised — people buy differently when they can touch things.

Who is actually stopping?

Note roughly who your customers are. Are they regulars walking by, or people who sought you out? Are they the same audience as your online customers?

What do people ask?

Every repeated question is information. If five people ask the same thing, that's either a gap in your signage, a pricing opportunity, or an untapped product idea. Write them down word for word.

When is traffic high vs. slow?

Track roughly how busy each day and hour is. Thursday at 2pm and Sunday at noon might be completely different markets. Understanding when your customers show up is foundational.

What makes someone put something down?

Watch what people pick up and set back down without buying. Is it price? Uncertainty about the product? Something about the display? This is harder to track online — use it.

How do people find you?

Ask! "Did you find us online or just walk by?" tells you whether your social posts are actually driving traffic or whether you're relying on foot traffic alone.

Your first 30 days — checklist

Phase 1 checklist

Complete these in your first month — before you try to optimize anything

  • Set up a simple tracking method — a notebook, spreadsheet, or app. Anything you'll actually use.
  • Identify your 3 best-selling items in your first month. These become your anchors.
  • Note what questions customers ask most. Write them down word for word — these are your best product development leads.
  • Observe foot traffic: what days and hours are consistently busiest?
  • Introduce yourself to your neighboring vendors. They have data you don't yet — use it.

A simple weekend tracker

You don't need fancy software. A shared Google Sheet or even a notebook works fine. The goal is to note a few things after each weekend — not to build a data dashboard. Update it Sunday evening while the weekend is fresh. It takes 5 minutes.

Three rules for Phase 1

  • 1

    Don't change two things at once. If you rearrange your display and lower your prices on the same weekend, you won't know which change caused any difference. In Phase 2, you'll test deliberately. For now, keep things consistent so your observations are clean.

  • 2

    Talk to customers — especially the ones who don't buy. A polite "thanks for stopping by, what brings you to the market today?" is all you need. The people who browse and leave without purchasing are often more informative than those who buy.

  • 3

    Give yourself permission for slow days. Rain, cold, and low foot traffic are normal parts of operating in the Pacific Northwest. A quiet Thursday doesn't mean the program isn't working — it might mean Thursday is not your day. That's useful to know. Write it down and keep going.

When it's slow

What to do with quiet time instead of feeling defeated

  • Stand outside your space and look at it the way a stranger walking by would. What draws the eye? What's confusing?
  • Write down three questions customers have asked this month. Does your display or signage answer any of them?
  • Chat with neighboring vendors. They notice things about your space that you can't see from inside it — and they have data you don't yet.
  • Post one behind-the-scenes photo or story to your social accounts. Slow in-person days are good content days.
  • Bring supplies and work on your product in the space. Customers are drawn to watching things being made.
  • Review your weekend log. Are any patterns starting to emerge, even after just a few weeks?
