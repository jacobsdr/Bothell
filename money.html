Money basics

Know if you're actually making money — and stay out of trouble

This section isn't about becoming an accountant. It's about making sure that two years from now, you actually know whether your Pop Shop made you money — and that you're not in trouble with the IRS because you mixed your business income with your grocery budget.

The one rule that matters most

Keep your business money completely separate from your personal money — from day one. Open a free business checking account this week. Move all business income into it. Pay all business expenses from it. That single habit makes everything else easier: tracking profit, filing taxes, applying for a loan, and proving your business is real.

Three concepts worth understanding

Revenue vs. profit — they are not the same thing

Revenue is every dollar that comes in. Profit is what's left after you subtract everything it cost to make and sell your product. Many first-time vendors look at their weekend sales total and feel good, without accounting for materials, time, fees, and the city's concession. Profit is the number that actually matters.

Revenue ($320) − Materials ($96) − Fees ($18) − Your time ($80) − Concession ($32) = Profit ($94)

Cost of goods — what it actually costs to make one item

Before you can price anything confidently, you need to know your cost to produce one unit — materials, packaging, and a fair estimate of your own time. A simple starting point: materials cost × 3 = minimum retail price. If your materials cost $8, don't sell for $12. Run the full number first.

Materials ($8) + Packaging ($1.50) + 20 min labor at $20/hr ($6.67) = Cost of goods ($16.17) → Minimum price ~$48

The 10% city concession

10% of your monthly on-site revenue goes to the city as your concession — this is effectively your rent. It's percentage-based, which is a benefit when you're starting out (low sales = low rent), but it needs to be factored into your profit calculation from day one.

$1,500 in on-site sales → $150 concession to the city that month

What counts as a cost

  • Materials and supplies to make your product
  • Packaging (bags, boxes, tissue paper, labels)
  • Payment processing fees (Square charges ~2.6% per swipe)
  • Pop Shop concession: 10% of monthly on-site revenue to the city
  • Display equipment, props, and signage you purchased
  • Your time — even if you don't pay yourself yet, track the hours
  • Certificate of Insurance (required — typically $300–$600/year)

Three ways to track — pick one and actually use it

Option A

Google Sheet

Free and flexible. Three columns: Date, Amount (positive for income, negative for expenses), Description. Update every Sunday. Takes 5 minutes.

Option B

Wave (free app)

Connects to your bank, categorizes automatically, and generates profit/loss reports — useful for loan or lease applications. Free at waveapps.com.

Option C

Square Dashboard

If you use Square, the free Dashboard tracks sales by day, item, and category. Pair with a simple expense log for the full picture.

Washington State — what you need to know

  • 1

    Washington State Business License. Required for any business operating in Washington. Apply at dor.wa.gov. Cost: $19 initial fee, $9–$71 per year depending on revenue. Takes about 10 minutes online.

  • 2

    City of Bothell endorsement. Required separately from the state license. Apply through the same Washington DOR portal under "city endorsements" — search for Bothell.

  • 3

    Certificate of Insurance. Required by the Pop Shops program, naming the City of Bothell as certificate holder. Basic general liability typically costs $300–$600/year. Get this before your first day.

  • 4

    Sales tax. Bothell's combined sales tax rate is 10.6% (verify at dor.wa.gov — rates can change). Configure Square to add this automatically before your first day. The money you collect is not yours — file and remit it quarterly to the state.

  • 5

    Keep receipts for everything. Materials, packaging, display supplies, printing — these are business expenses that reduce your taxable income. A folder in Google Drive is enough to start.

Common mistake — spending your sales tax

Sales tax collected from customers is not your money — it belongs to the state. Every time you make a sale, mentally set aside 10.6% of it in your business account. You'll never be caught short at filing time.

Monthly money check-in — 20 minutes, once a month

Ask yourself these questions every month

  • What was my total on-site Pop Shop revenue this month?
  • What did I spend on materials and supplies?
  • What were my payment processing fees?
  • What is my 10% concession amount owed to the city?
  • What was my actual profit (revenue minus all of the above)?
  • Am I pricing high enough to make the profit I need?
  • Is there one product I should stop making because it doesn't cover its costs?
