Money basics

Know if you're actually making money — and stay out of trouble

You don't need accounting software or a business degree. You need three things: a way to track what's coming in and going out, a separate bank account, and a basic understanding of Washington's rules for retail sellers.

The one rule that matters most

Keep your business money completely separate from your personal money — from day one. This single habit makes everything else easier: tracking profit, filing taxes, applying for loans, and proving your business is real. It costs nothing to open a second checking account and it saves you enormous headaches later.

Three concepts worth understanding

Revenue vs. profit — they are not the same thing

Revenue is every dollar that comes in. Profit is what's left after you subtract everything it cost to make and sell your product — materials, fees, packaging, your time. Many first-time vendors look at their weekend sales total and feel good, without accounting for what they spent. Profit is the number that actually matters.

Revenue ($320 weekend sales) − Costs ($140 materials + $30 fees) = Profit ($150)

Cost of goods — what it actually costs to make one item

Before you can price anything confidently, you need to know what it costs to produce one unit — materials, packaging, and a fair estimate of your own labor time. If you don't know your cost of goods, you might be pricing at a loss without realizing it.

Materials ($8) + Packaging ($1.50) + 20 min labor at $20/hr ($6.67) = Cost of goods ($16.17)

Margin — how much you keep on each sale

Margin is the percentage of a sale price that is profit. A healthy retail margin for handmade goods is typically 50–70%. If your cost of goods is $16 and you sell for $40, your margin is 60% — meaning you keep $24 of every $40 sale. Knowing your margin tells you how many units you need to sell to cover your costs each weekend.

($40 price − $16 cost) ÷ $40 price = 60% margin

Washington State — what you need to know

  • 1

    You likely need a Washington State business license. If you're selling goods and making more than $12,000 per year, a state business license is required. It costs $90 for two years and takes about 10 minutes online. If you're below that threshold it's still worth getting — it makes you look credible and is required if you ever want a business bank account.

  • 2

    You are required to collect and remit sales tax on retail sales in Washington. Washington's state sales tax is 6.5%, plus a local rate for Bothell (currently around 3.6% — verify at dor.wa.gov). You collect it from customers at the point of sale and send it to the state quarterly. Your payment processor (Square, Venmo, etc.) can often handle the calculation automatically.

  • 3

    Keep receipts for everything you buy for the business. Materials, packaging, display supplies, printing — these are all business expenses that reduce your taxable income. A simple folder (physical or in Google Drive) of receipts is all you need to start.

  • 4

    You don't need to form an LLC right away. Many small vendors operate as a sole proprietor (just yourself, under your own name or a trade name) to start. It's simpler and costs nothing. An LLC becomes worth considering once you have consistent revenue and want liability protection — but it's not the first step.

Common mistake

Spending your sales tax money before you remit it to the state. Sales tax collected from customers is not your money — it belongs to the state. A simple habit: every time you make a sale, mentally set aside 10% of it in your business account for tax. You'll never be caught short at filing time.
