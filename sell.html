How to actually sell

Selling in person feels different.
Here's how to make it natural.

Most vendors who come to Pop Shops have built their business online. What many haven't done is stand in front of a stranger and talk about their product. This isn't about sales tactics — it's about reframing what in-person selling actually is.

The hosting mindset

The vendors who are most effective in person aren't the ones with the slickest pitch. They're the ones who make customers feel comfortable, curious, and welcome. Think of your space less like a store and more like your home — your job is making guests feel at ease, not closing a transaction. And one practical note: stand near the entrance of your space, not behind your table. Your visible presence is part of the display.

How to greet people — what to say and what to avoid

Customer walks in or pauses outside

Say this

"Hey, welcome! Let me know if you have any questions."

Not this

"Can I help you find something?"

Warm and zero-pressure. "Can I help you?" puts them on the spot and is easy to deflect with "just looking."

Customer is looking at a specific item

Say this

"That one's really popular — people love it for [specific reason]. Want to take a closer look?"

Not this

"That's one of my best sellers!" [then silence]

You're adding useful information and inviting engagement — not just making a claim and waiting.

Customer walks by slowly but doesn't stop

Say this

"We're open! Feel free to come in and browse."

Not this

[Nothing — silence reads as closed or unwelcoming]

A brief, friendly invitation costs nothing and catches people who were curious but uncertain.

Someone seems interested but quiet

Say this

"I make everything myself — happy to tell you about anything you're curious about."

Not this

[Hovering silently, or asking "are you going to buy something?"]

Opens the door to a conversation without creating pressure. The human connection is what in-person retail has over online.

Customer says "I'll think about it"

Say this

"Of course! We're here through Sunday if you want to come back — and that one tends to go quickly on weekends, just so you know."

Not this

"Are you sure? It's one of our most popular items."

Don't chase. Remove pressure and keep the door open. Sometimes people leave and come back ten minutes later with a friend.

Customer says "I need to check with my partner"

Say this

"Totally. If it helps, we do accept returns within [X days] — so there's no pressure to decide today."

Not this

"Well, I'm sure they'd love it!"

Removes friction without dismissing the concern. Gives them a concrete reason to feel comfortable deciding.

Reading buying signals

Buying signals
Scanning the whole space quickly
Picking up a specific item more than once
Not making eye contact
Asking about price unprompted
Moving through fast without stopping
Asking "do you have this in..." (size, color, qty)
Looking at everything generally
Showing the item to someone they're with
Politely nodding at your greeting
Standing in front of one display for 30+ seconds

When you see buying signals, don't pitch harder — make the decision easier. Offer information, remove friction, and let them close themselves.

Your 30-second product story

One thing worth preparing in advance

A good 30-second story has three parts

What it is (5 sec) The simplest possible description. "This is a soy candle with a cedar and bergamot scent."
Why you made it (15 sec) The human part. "I started making these during the pandemic when I wanted my home to smell like being outside — I couldn't find anything like it, so I made it."
Why it's right for them (10 sec) Connect it to their situation. "If you find synthetic scents overwhelming, this one's different — it's light enough to have going all day."

Exercise — do this before your first weekend

Write your 30-second story for your top 3 items: what it is / why you made it / why it's right for them. Practice saying each one out loud. It sounds simple but most people stumble the first few times someone asks. Having a natural, rehearsed answer is the difference between a confident conversation and an awkward one.

Common pitfalls for first-time in-person sellers

Pitfall: Apologizing for your prices

If you say "it's $45... I know that's a lot" you've undercut your own product before the customer has decided anything. State prices confidently. If a price needs explaining, explain the value — not the cost.

Pitfall: Hovering

Following a customer around or watching them closely makes them uncomfortable and shortens their visit. Greet them, offer one piece of context, then give them space. Be available, not omnipresent.

Pitfall: Being on your phone when someone walks in

You have about two seconds to acknowledge a new visitor. Mid-scroll when someone enters sets the tone slightly wrong for the whole interaction. Keep your phone face-down or out of sight during open hours.

Pitfall: Treating browsers as a waste of time

People who browse without buying are still forming an impression of you and your business. They might follow you on Instagram, mention you to a friend, or come back next month with someone else. Treat everyone like they matter — because they do.
