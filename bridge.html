Bridging online to in-person

Turn your existing followers into in-person visitors

You already have an audience. The question is how to get them to show up in person — and then use your physical presence to strengthen the online relationship at the same time.

Why this matters for your specific situation

Unlike a brand-new business, you're not starting from zero. You have followers, customers, and credibility online. That's an asset most pop-up vendors don't have. Your job isn't to build an audience from scratch — it's to activate the one you already have and give them a reason to cross from the screen to your door.

The announcement week — what to post before you open

Monday — the announcementPost with a photo of your space or product

"Exciting news — [Business Name] now has a physical home! I've joined Pop Shops on Main in downtown Bothell, open Thu–Sun noon–6pm. If you've been following along online and always wanted to see the work in person, now's your chance. Come say hi — I'd love to meet you."

Tag @CityofBothell and use #BothellWA and #PopShopsOnMain so local people can discover you too.

Thursday — the reminderPost morning of your first open day

"We're open today! Pop Shops on Main, downtown Bothell, noon–6. I'll be here all weekend. Come see [specific product] in person — it's one of those things that's hard to fully appreciate in photos."

Mention one specific product. Vague posts ("come check us out!") underperform posts that give people something concrete to be curious about.

Behind the scenes — any dayDuring setup or a quiet moment

"Setting up for the weekend — here's what the space looks like right now. I'm still figuring out the display [photo of in-progress setup]. Come see the final version this weekend!"

Behind-the-scenes content performs well because it makes followers feel included in the process. It also creates curiosity about what the "finished" version looks like.

After a good weekendSunday evening or Monday

"Incredible first weekend at Pop Shops. Met so many of you in person for the first time — thank you for coming out. [Specific observation, e.g. 'The [product] sold out by Saturday afternoon']. Back again this Thursday. See you then."

Closing the loop with a recap builds momentum for the next weekend and shows online followers that real things are happening worth showing up for.

Building a simple customer list

You don't need a POS system or email software to start. The goal is to capture contact information from people who visit so you can reach them directly — without relying on social media algorithms.

1

Put out a simple sign-up card or tablet

A handwritten card that says "Sign up for new arrivals and weekend updates" with a line for name and email is enough. Or a tablet open to a Google Form. People who are genuinely interested will sign up — you don't need to push it.

2

Store contacts in a Google Sheet

No software needed yet. A simple spreadsheet with name, email, and date they signed up is all you need. When you eventually move to an email tool like Mailchimp (free up to 500 contacts), you can import this list directly.

3

Email once a month — not more

A short monthly email ("Here's what's new at the pop-up this month") keeps you in mind without burning out your list. Include one product highlight, your hours, and a link to your online shop. That's it.

Using what you learn in-person to improve your online presence

Physical retail tells you things your online analytics can't

  • The questions customers ask in person are your best product description and FAQ copy — write them down and add them to your listings
  • The products people pick up and examine most are your best candidates for better photography online
  • The stories customers tell about why they're buying (gifts, self-treat, specific occasion) are your best social content angles
  • If customers are surprised by the quality when they see it in person, your online photos aren't doing the product justice — that's a fixable problem
  • Repeat visitors are your most loyal customers — note who comes back and what they buy, because those people are also likely your best online advocates
